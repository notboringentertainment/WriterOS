# Treatment Best Practices

Reference guidance for the WriterOS Treatment surface. This document is the stable
source the Treatment Edit View, prompt copy, and passage templates derive from.
It exists so future agents and contributors share one understanding of what a
treatment is and how WriterOS presents it.

## What a treatment is

A treatment tells the full story in cinematic prose — the whole film as a
continuous read, before or alongside the script. It is longer and more vivid
than a synopsis and less mechanical than an outline. A producer, collaborator,
or studio reader should be able to read a treatment and feel the movie.

In WriterOS the four planning surfaces have distinct jobs:

- **Synopsis** — the short pitch.
- **Outline** — the structural skeleton: what happens, in order.
- **Story Bible** — the reference canon: world, characters, rules.
- **Treatment** — the readable movie: the whole story as cinematic prose.

Treatment is the bridge between Outline's skeleton and the script's pages, and
the single best artifact for giving an AI specialist the full dramatic flow.

## Craft sources

This guidance draws on widely used industry standards, not a single methodology:

- **Final Draft** — outlines, treatments, and scriptments are distinct document
  types with distinct purposes; do not collapse them.
- **BBC Writers** — pitches and treatments should reveal the major twists, not
  withhold the story. A treatment spoils itself on purpose.
- **UCLA treatment guidance** — third-person, present tense; visible and
  hearable action; minimal unfilmable interiority; no camera directions.
- **General story-development frameworks** — treatment, outline, Truby, and beat
  sheets all describe the same dramatic shapes (setup, pressure, turns,
  collapse, resolution) in different vocabularies.

## The WriterOS plain-language principle

WriterOS hides industry jargon. Craft frameworks are translated into plain
questions any writer can answer. The Edit View must never expose terms like
"inciting incident," "dark night of the soul," "midpoint," or any Save the Cat
beat name. Save the Cat is only one possible source. The questions carry the
craft; the labels stay human.

Examples of the translation:

- not "inciting incident" → "What pulls them into the story?"
- not "all is lost" → "How does the pressure build and turn?"
- not "resolution / final image" → "How does it resolve?"

## Recommended structure

A WriterOS treatment is prose-first. The structured fields are scaffolding
around the prose; the prose passages are the document.

1. **Logline** — the story in one sentence: who, goal, pressure, stakes, hook.
2. **The promise** — the story-level identity:
   - what kind of story it is (genre, mood, closest comparison)
   - the truth underneath it (theme)
   - what the audience should feel by the end (emotional payoff)
3. **Who carries it** — the main characters: role, what they want, what they
   need, how they change.
4. **The story flow** — the whole film in present-tense prose, told in four
   moves: how it opens, what pulls them in, how the pressure builds and turns,
   how it resolves.
5. **Story passages** — focused prose passages beyond the main flow. Optional,
   added as the story needs them. Common kinds:
   - a character passage — one person's journey end to end
   - a place or world passage — a setting and its rules
   - a major turn — one moment where the story changes direction
   - a big sequence — one extended stretch as it plays on screen
   - a side thread — a secondary storyline and how it pays off
   - a free passage — anything else the story needs
6. **Texture** — the sensory and cinematic expression of the promise:
   atmosphere scene to scene, the visual world, recurring images and motifs,
   sound, pacing, genre rules, comparisons and references.
7. **Open questions** — decisions still unsettled, grouped by story, character,
   world or mythology, and production.

## Promise vs Texture

These two sections both deal in feeling and must not overlap:

- **The promise** is story-level — the kind of story, the theme, the emotional
  payoff. What the audience is signing up for.
- **Texture** is craft-level — the sustained sensory experience that expresses
  that promise. The atmosphere, images, and sound the film actually carries.

Ask each feeling question once, in one place.

## Edit View behavior

- Prose-first: the writer should be able to write long-form text comfortably.
- Sections should be collapsible — treatments get long.
- Story passages are added from a template menu so the page is never blank;
  each template inserts a strong heading and editable placeholder guidance.
- AI production notes, if present, stay separate from the prose.

## Document View behavior

- Render as a clean, readable treatment.
- No form chrome.
- No agent notes mixed into the prose.

## How specialists use a treatment

- **Alex** is the primary helper — treatment bridges concept, structure,
  process, and draft readiness.
- **Sam** can compress a treatment into a synopsis or logline.
- **Oliver** can map treatment prose back to structure.

Strong, descriptive passage headings ("Character passage," "Place or world
passage," "Major turn") make a treatment legible to every specialist without
any extra routing machinery.
